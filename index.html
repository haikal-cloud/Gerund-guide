The World of Gerunds

An interactive guide to understanding the verbs that act like nouns.

What is a Gerund?

A gerund is a special verb form that always ends in -ing and, most importantly, functions as a noun in a sentence. It names an activity or a state of being.

Think of it this way: It looks like a verb, but it acts like a noun.

The Four Core Functions

A gerund can play several roles in a sentence. Click the tabs below to explore each function with examples.

As the Subject of a Sentence

The gerund is the main idea the sentence is about.

  • Running is my favorite hobby.
  • Learning a new language can be challenging.

As the Object of a Verb

Many common verbs are directly followed by a gerund.

  • I enjoy swimming in the ocean.
  • She suggested going to the cinema.

As the Object of a Preposition

If a verb follows a preposition (like *at, for, about*), it must be a gerund.

  • I'm good at playing chess.
  • Thanks for helping me.

After Certain Expressions

Some fixed phrases in English are always followed by a gerund.

  • It's no use waiting in long queues.
  • It's no use crying over spilled milk.

Gerund vs. Present Participle

This is a common point of confusion. Both end in -ing, but their jobs in a sentence are completely different. Here's a clear comparison.

Gerund (Acts as a NOUN)

It names an activity.

  • Running is good exercise.
  • Here, "Running" is the 'thing' that is good for you.

Present Participle (Acts as an ADJECTIVE or VERB)

It describes a noun or is part of a continuous verb tense.

  • The running water was cold.
  • Here, "running" describes the water.
  • He is running a marathon.
  • Here, "is running" is the action he is doing right now.
